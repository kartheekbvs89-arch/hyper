Assignment Overview

In this assignment you will empirically quantify the speedup that Hyperband provides over plain RandomizedSearchCV on a realistic tabular-classification problem. You will set up an XGBClassifier on the load_digits dataset, use n_estimators as the multi-fidelity budget axis, run HalvingRandomSearchCV with the same total compute budget as a RandomizedSearchCV baseline, and dissect the bracket-by-bracket elimination that Hyperband performs. The bonus task asks you to vary η and explain how the exploration-exploitation trade-off shifts.

Plan for 3–4 hours of focused work. Roughly: 30 minutes to set up the dataset and baseline, 1 hour to get HalvingRandomSearchCV running and producing a cv_results_ DataFrame you can introspect, 1 hour to build the bracket-elimination plot and the comparison table, and 30–60 minutes on the bonus. If you get stuck on the bracket introspection, re-read Section 6 of the Hyperband article — the iter and candidate_id columns of cv_results_ are the keys.

Dataset & Model

We use sklearn.datasets.load_digits — 1,797 samples, 64 pixel features, 10 classes. The model is xgboost.XGBClassifier with n_estimators as the budget axis. n_estimators is a natural choice because XGBoost is additive: n_estimators=10 gives you a strictly cheaper, slightly worse version of the same model that n_estimators=300 would produce, which is exactly the monotone budget-quality relationship Hyperband requires.

Task 5 (Bonus) — Vary η and Explain the Trade-off

Re-run Task 3 with factor (η) set to 3, 4, and 6. Keep max_resources=300 and min_resources=10 the same. For each η, record the total number of unique candidates explored, the number of SHA rounds, and the final test accuracy. Write a 4-5 sentence explanation of how the exploration-exploitation trade-off shifts as η changes.

Hint: what to look for

Large η means more aggressive elimination per round — each round keeps fewer candidates, so the total number of unique candidates per bracket is larger but most of them get only one or two rounds before being killed. Small η keeps more candidates per round, so each candidate gets more chances to recover from a bad early score, but the total number of unique candidates is smaller. The accuracy should be relatively insensitive to η in the [3, 6] range; the wall-clock time and the number of survivors per round are the things that change visibly.
