1. The Multi-Fidelity Insight

Every search method you have seen so far — GridSearchCV, RandomizedSearchCV, Bayesian optimization, TPE, CMA-ES — shares the same hidden assumption: each candidate configuration is trained to completion, evaluated once, and then either kept or discarded. The evaluation cost is the same for every configuration, regardless of whether it ends up being the best or the worst. This is the single-fidelity regime, and on cheap models (logistic regression, small random forests) it is fine. On deep models — where one configuration can take hours or days to train — it is ruinous.

The empirical observation that breaks the single-fidelity assumption is this: most bad configurations reveal themselves early. A learning rate of 1.0 will diverge within the first few hundred steps; an over-regularized model will already be underfitting after one epoch; a terrible architecture will not suddenly become good at epoch 90. If you are willing to look at partial-training signals, you can identify and discard the obviously-bad candidates when they have used only a tiny fraction of the full budget, and redirect that saved compute toward configurations that still have a chance of being the best.

This is the multi-fidelity idea. Instead of treating each configuration's score as a single number, you treat it as a curve of scores against training budget. The search algorithm queries the curve at small budgets for many candidates, then progressively concentrates compute on the survivors. The total compute spent is bounded, but the effective number of fully-trained models you get to compare is much larger — because you never fully train the ones that were already losing at the small-budget checkpoints. Hyperband (Li, Jamieson, Rostamizadeh, Talwalkar 2017) is the cleanest and most influential formalization of this idea, and it is the algorithm we will spend most of this article on.

📌
Multi-fidelity is older than Hyperband

The multi-fidelity idea appears in many forms: the racing algorithms of Maron & Moore (1997), the bandit-based model selection of Jamieson & Talwalkar (2016), and the optimization-on-a-budget framework of Swersky et al. (2014). Hyperband's contribution was to (a) prove a non-trivial theoretical guarantee, (b) propose a hyperparameter-free schedule that matches the best possible one up to a log factor, and (c) provide an extremely simple implementation. The combination is what made it the standard tool it is today.

2. Successive Halving (SHA): The Building Block

Hyperband is built on a simpler algorithm called Successive Halving (SHA). The recipe is exactly what the name suggests. You start with n candidate configurations, each trained for a small budget of r = R/n resources (where R is the per-candidate budget you would have spent in single-fidelity mode). You score them all, throw away the bottom 1 - 1/η fraction, and give each survivor η × more budget. Repeat until only one candidate is left, which has consumed the full budget R.

The parameter η (eta) controls the trade-off between exploration and exploitation inside a single SHA run. η = 3 is the default and is almost always the right choice: each round keeps the top third and triples the budget, so a configuration has to be in the top third at every checkpoint to survive to the end. Smaller η (like 2) keeps more candidates per round — more exploration, slower convergence. Larger η (like 6) is more aggressive — fewer survivors, faster convergence, but more risk of throwing out a config that would have caught up with more budget.

Figure 1 — One Successive Halving bracket with η=3, n=9, R=1 unit of budget. Each round eliminates the bottom 2/3 of candidates and gives each survivor 3× more compute. The total compute used is 3R, vs 9R for naive single-fidelity search over 9 candidates — a 67% saving.

The total compute consumed by one SHA run is a geometric series: n · (R/n) + (n/η) · (ηR/n) + (n/η²) · (η²R/n) + ... — at each round you spend the same total budget R, and there are log_η(n) rounds. So the total is R · log_η(n) + R, a logarithmic overhead compared to single-fidelity search's nR. That is the source of Hyperband's "5-10x speedup" reputation: it is mathematically the difference between linear and logarithmic scaling with the number of candidates.

successive_halving_from_scratch.py
# ─── successive_halving_from_scratch.py ─────────────────────────────────────────
# A minimal, dependency-free reimplementation of Successive Halving so the
# algorithm's structure is crystal clear. Compare this to the production
# implementation inside sklearn.experimental.enable_successive_halving_search.

import numpy as np
from sklearn.datasets import load_digits
from sklearn.ensemble import RandomForestClassifier
from sklearn.model_selection import StratifiedKFold, cross_val_score

def sample_config(rng):
    return {
        "n_estimators":     int(rng.choice([50, 100, 200, 400])),
        "max_depth":        int(rng.choice([4, 8, 12, 16, None])),
        "max_features":     rng.choice(["sqrt", "log2", 0.5]),
        "min_samples_leaf": int(rng.choice([1, 2, 4, 8])),
    }

def evaluate(config, X, y, budget):
    """`budget` = n_estimators actually trained. Lower = cheaper, noisier signal."""
    cfg = dict(config)
    cfg["n_estimators"] = max(5, int(budget))
    clf = RandomForestClassifier(random_state=0, n_jobs=-1, **cfg)
    cv = StratifiedKFold(n_splits=3, shuffle=True, random_state=0)
    return cross_val_score(clf, X, y, cv=cv, scoring="accuracy").mean()

def successive_halving(n, R, eta, X, y, seed=0):
    rng = np.random.default_rng(seed)
    configs = [sample_config(rng) for _ in range(n)]
    r = R / n                                       # initial per-candidate budget
    s = int(np.floor(np.log(n) / np.log(eta)))      # number of halvings
    print(f"SHA: n={n}, R={R}, eta={eta}, halvings={s}, initial r={r:.1f}")
    for round_idx in range(s + 1):
        scores = [evaluate(c, X, y, r) for c in configs]
        order = np.argsort(scores)[::-1]
        keep = max(1, int(np.floor(len(configs) / eta)))
        print(f"  Round {round_idx}: {len(configs)} candidates, "
              f"budget r={r:.1f}, best score={scores[order[0]]:.4f}, "
              f"keeping top {keep}")
        configs = [configs[i] for i in order[:keep]]
        r = r * eta
    return configs[0], scores[order[0]]

if __name__ == "__main__":
    X, y = load_digits(return_X_y=True)
    best_cfg, best_score = successive_halving(n=27, R=400, eta=3, X=X, y=y)
    print(f"\nBest config: {best_cfg}\nBest score:  {best_score:.4f}")

Run this on the digits dataset and you will see exactly the elimination pattern from Figure 1: round 0 evaluates 27 cheap configs at R/27 ≈ 15 trees each, round 1 keeps the best 9 at 45 trees, round 2 keeps the best 3 at 135 trees, and the final round trains the winner to 400 trees. The whole loop runs in a fraction of the time that 27 independent full-budget evaluations would have taken.

3. The Problem With Plain SHA

Successive Halving has one uncomfortable parameter: the initial number of candidates n. Pick n too large and you spend almost all your compute on the first round, with very little left to distinguish the survivors — this is pure exploration, and the final winner may not be much better than the early-round top candidate. Pick n too small and you spend most of your compute on a tiny set of candidates that you could have just fully trained anyway — this is pure exploitation, and you may never even sample a good configuration.

This is a classic exploration-vs-exploitation dilemma, and SHA forces you to commit to one point on the trade-off curve before you have any data. The fix is to not commit — run multiple SHA brackets with different values of n, and let them collectively cover the trade-off. This is Hyperband.

💡
Read SHA as a "ladder"

Think of one SHA run as a ladder: at the bottom you have many short ladders (lots of candidates, each with a small budget), and at the top you have one tall ladder (one candidate with the full budget). The question Hyperband asks is: how many ladders, and at what heights, should I place to best cover the range of possible best-config-budget trade-offs? The answer is: place ladders at every feasible height, with budgets allocated so the total is exactly R · s_max.

4. Hyperband: Multiple Brackets, No Tuning Knob

Hyperband runs s_max + 1 SHA brackets, where s_max is determined by the total budget B and the maximum per-candidate budget R. Bracket s uses initial resources r = R · η^(-s) and starts with n = ⌈(B/R) · η^s / (s+1)⌉ candidates. The total compute used by bracket s is exactly B, independent of s — so all brackets contribute equally to the total compute, and the algorithm spends the same amount of money on exploration-heavy brackets (large s) as on exploitation-heavy brackets (small s).

$$ s_{max} = \left\lfloor \log_\eta\!\left(\frac{B}{R}\right) \right\rfloor, \quad n_s = \left\lceil \frac{B}{R} \cdot \frac{\eta^s}{s+1} \right\rceil, \quad r_s = R \cdot \eta^{-s} $$
Eq. 1 — Hyperband bracket schedule. s_max brackets total; bracket s starts with n_s candidates at r_s resources each.

Read each symbol carefully. $s_$ is the number of brackets minus one — it is determined by the ratio of total budget B to per-candidate budget R and by η. $n_s$ is the number of candidates you start bracket s with; it grows exponentially with s because the η^s factor dominates. $r_s$ is the per-candidate budget at round 0 of bracket s; it shrinks exponentially with s as η^(-s). The product n_s · r_s stays roughly constant at B/(s+1), which is what makes each bracket's total compute equal.

The intuition for why this works: bracket s=0 starts with very few candidates each at the full budget R — pure exploitation, equivalent to plain random search over a small set. Bracket s=s_max starts with many candidates each at the smallest budget R/η^s_max — pure exploration, lots of cheap looks, no time to distinguish between survivors. By running both and every bracket in between, Hyperband hedges against whichever regime the problem happens to favor. There is no n to tune — the schedule is fully determined by B, R, and η.

🔥
The theoretical guarantee

Li et al. prove that Hyperband finds a configuration within a factor of O(log n) of the best possible, using total compute that is O(n log n) — versus the O(n^2) that naive search would need to find the same configuration. That log-factor is the formal version of the '5-10x speedup' practitioners observe. The guarantee is with respect to the infinite-data best configuration in the candidate pool, not the true Bayes-optimal model, so it does not say anything about model family choice — but it does say that given a pool of candidates, Hyperband is essentially the best you can do.

5. BOHB: Bayesian Optimization + Hyperband

Hyperband samples its initial candidates uniformly at random. That is the same first step as RandomizedSearchCV, and it has the same drawback: a substantial fraction of the budget is wasted on configurations in obviously-bad regions of the space before any of them have been trained enough to identify the good region. Bayesian optimization methods like TPE and Gaussian Processes address this by learning from completed trials — but they assume each trial is fully trained, which defeats the multi-fidelity speedup.

BOHB (Falkner, Klein, Hutter 2018) combines the two. Inside each Hyperband bracket, instead of sampling the initial candidates uniformly, it uses TPE (or any Bayesian optimization method) to propose them, conditioned on the results of all previous brackets. Configurations that did well in earlier brackets inform the proposals for later brackets, so the search concentrates on the promising region of the space while still using the multi-fidelity budget schedule to avoid wasting compute on bad candidates. Empirically, BOHB finds better configurations than vanilla Hyperband in the same compute budget, especially when the search space is high-dimensional and only a small fraction of it is any good.

bohb_with_optuna.py
# ─── bohb_with_optuna.py ───────────────────────────────────────────────────────
# A simplified, instructive BOHB-style loop: TPE (Optuna) proposes configs,
# and a Hyperband-style schedule decides which configs to keep / kill.
# For the real production BOHB implementation, see github.com/automl/HpBandster.

import optuna
import numpy as np
from sklearn.datasets import load_digits
from sklearn.ensemble import RandomForestClassifier
from sklearn.model_selection import StratifiedKFold, cross_val_score

def objective(trial):
    cfg = {
        "n_estimators":     trial.suggest_categorical("n_estimators", [50, 100, 200, 400]),
        "max_depth":        trial.suggest_categorical("max_depth", [4, 8, 12, 16, 32]),
        "max_features":     trial.suggest_categorical("max_features", ["sqrt", "log2", 0.5]),
        "min_samples_leaf": trial.suggest_int("min_samples_leaf", 1, 8, log=True),
    }
    # The Hyperband schedule is encoded in trial.user_attrs via a custom sampler
    # / pruning callback (not shown here). For each trial, Optuna reports
    # intermediate scores at budgets [R/eta^s, R/eta^(s-1), ..., R]; the
    # SuccessiveHalvingPruner kills trials whose intermediate score falls
    # below the median of all trials at the same budget.
    X, y = load_digits(return_X_y=True)
    cv = StratifiedKFold(n_splits=3, shuffle=True, random_state=0)
    for budget in [10, 30, 90, 270]:   # SHA-like budget ramp, eta = 3
        clf = RandomForestClassifier(
            n_estimators=budget, n_jobs=-1, random_state=0, **{k: v for k, v in cfg.items() if k != "n_estimators"}
        )
        score = cross_val_score(clf, X, y, cv=cv, scoring="accuracy").mean()
        trial.report(score, step=budget)
        if trial.should_prune():
            raise optuna.TrialPruned()
    return score

study = optuna.create_study(
    direction="maximize",
    pruner=optuna.pruners.SuccessiveHalvingPruner(min_resource=10, reduction_factor=3),
    sampler=optuna.samplers.TPESampler(seed=0),
)
study.optimize(objective, n_trials=60, show_progress_bar=False)
print(f"Best score: {study.best_value:.4f}")
print(f"Best params: {study.best_params}")
print(f"Trials pruned: {sum(1 for t in study.trials if t.state == optuna.trial.TrialState.PRUNED)}/60")

The Optuna snippet above is not literally BOHB — it uses TPE for proposal and SuccessiveHalvingPruner for the budget schedule, which is the same architectural combination. The original BOHB implementation in HpBandSter uses the same idea with a slightly different bookkeeping. Both achieve the same empirical benefit: roughly the speed of Hyperband plus the sample efficiency of Bayesian optimization.

6. scikit-learn: HalvingRandomSearchCV & HalvingGridSearchCV

Since scikit-learn 1.0, Hyperband-style search is available out of the box via HalvingRandomSearchCV and HalvingGridSearchCV in the sklearn.experimental module. The API mirrors the regular RandomizedSearchCV / GridSearchCV almost exactly — the only new parameters are factor (η), resource (which hyperparameter is the budget axis), max_resources, min_resources, and aggressive_elimination.

The default resource is 'n_estimators', which is exactly right for tree ensembles: RandomForestClassifier and GradientBoostingClassifier both expose n_estimators, and reducing it gives a strictly cheaper, slightly noisier version of the same model. For deep learning you would not use sklearn's Halving classes directly (you would use Ray Tune or Optuna); for tabular ML they are the right tool.

sklearn_halving_search.py
# ─── sklearn_halving_search.py ─────────────────────────────────────────────────
# HalvingRandomSearchCV and HalvingGridSearchCV shipped in scikit-learn 1.0
# under the experimental module. Enable them with this one import line:

from sklearn.experimental import enable_successive_halving_search  # noqa: F401
from sklearn.model_selection import HalvingRandomSearchCV, HalvingGridSearchCV
from sklearn.ensemble import RandomForestClassifier
from sklearn.datasets import load_digits
import numpy as np
import time

X, y = load_digits(return_X_y=True)

# ─── 1. Define the search space ───────────────────────────────────────────────
param_distributions = {
    "n_estimators":     [50, 100, 200, 400],
    "max_depth":        [4, 8, 12, 16, None],
    "max_features":     ["sqrt", "log2", 0.5],
    "min_samples_leaf": [1, 2, 4, 8],
}

# ─── 2. HalvingRandomSearchCV ─────────────────────────────────────────────────
#  factor=3 means eta=3: each round keeps top 1/3 and triples the budget.
#  resource='n_estimators' means n_estimators is the budget axis (default).
#  min_resources=20 means smallest budget = 20 trees in round 0.
#  aggressive_elimination=False keeps the standard SHA schedule.
base = RandomForestClassifier(random_state=42, n_jobs=-1)
halving = HalvingRandomSearchCV(
    estimator=base,
    param_distributions=param_distributions,
    factor=3,                       # eta
    resource="n_estimators",
    max_resources=400,
    min_resources=20,
    cv=3,
    scoring="accuracy",
    n_candidates="exhaust",        # use as many candidates as the budget allows
    random_state=42,
    n_jobs=1,                       # the CV loop itself is sequential
    verbose=1,
)

t0 = time.perf_counter()
halving.fit(X, y)
t1 = time.perf_counter()
print(f"\nHalvingRandomSearchCV wall-clock: {t1 - t0:.1f}s")
print(f"Best score: {halving.best_score_:.4f}")
print(f"Best params: {halving.best_params_}")

# ─── 3. Inspect the bracket-by-bracket results ────────────────────────────────
# cv_results_ has one row per (candidate, round) pair. Use this to plot the
# elimination sequence.
import pandas as pd
results = pd.DataFrame(halving.cv_results_)
print("\nRound-by-round summary (mean_fit_time and mean_test_score):")
summary = (results.groupby("iter")
                  .agg(n_candidates=("candidate_id", "nunique"),
                       mean_fit_time=("mean_fit_time", "sum"),
                       best_score=("mean_test_score", "max"))
                  .reset_index()
                  .rename(columns={"iter": "round"}))
print(summary.to_string(index=False))

The cv_results_ DataFrame has one row per (candidate, round) pair, which means you can inspect exactly which configurations were eliminated in which round and at what score. This is the data you need for the bracket-by-bracket elimination plot that the assignment asks for. The iter column tells you the round number; mean_test_score is the per-round score; grouping by iter and counting unique candidate_id values gives the bracket shape.

⚠️
Halving*SearchCV is still experimental

As of scikit-learn 1.3, the Halving classes live in sklearn.experimental and require the from sklearn.experimental import enable_successive_halving_search import line to be activated. The API is stable but the maintainers reserve the right to make breaking changes before promoting the classes out of experimental. For production code, pin your scikit-learn version. For tutorial code, just include the import line.

7. Ray Tune: Hyperband for Deep Learning

scikit-learn's Halving classes assume a single-machine, scikit-learn-style estimator. For deep learning, where the budget axis is "epochs" or "steps" and training is naturally distributed, Ray Tune is the dominant framework. It ships ASHAScheduler (Async Successive Halving — the distributed variant where candidates are evaluated concurrently and slow ones are killed the moment their partial score falls below the median, without waiting for the whole bracket to finish) and HyperbandScheduler (the synchronous multi-bracket version closer to the paper).

For most practical work, ASHA is preferred over synchronous Hyperband: it has the same theoretical guarantees but much better cluster utilization, because workers never sit idle waiting for slower peers to finish their current round. Pair ASHA with a TPE-based search algorithm (HyperOptSearch or OptunaSearch) and you have a BOHB-equivalent setup with one or two function calls.

ray_tune_hyperband.py
# ─── ray_tune_hyperband.py ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
# Ray Tune ships first-class Hyperband and ASHA (Async Successive Halving)
# schedulers. ASHA is the distributed variant: candidates are evaluated
# concurrently, and slow ones are killed the moment their partial score
# is below the median, without waiting for the whole bracket to finish.

import torch
import torch.nn as nn
from ray import tune
from ray.tune.schedulers import ASHAScheduler
from ray.tune.search.hyperopt import HyperOptSearch

# ─── 1. Define a trainable: a small CNN on Fashion-MNIST ──────────────────────
def train_fn(config):
    # config has: lr, batch_size, conv1_channels, conv2_channels, dropout
    model = nn.Sequential(
        nn.Conv2d(1, config["conv1_channels"], 3, padding=1),
        nn.ReLU(), nn.MaxPool2d(2),
        nn.Conv2d(config["conv1_channels"], config["conv2_channels"], 3, padding=1),
        nn.ReLU(), nn.MaxPool2d(2),
        nn.Flatten(),
        nn.Linear(config["conv2_channels"] * 7 * 7, 64), nn.ReLU(),
        nn.Dropout(config["dropout"]),
        nn.Linear(64, 10),
    )
    opt = torch.optim.Adam(model.parameters(), lr=config["lr"])
    loss_fn = nn.CrossEntropyLoss()
    # ... data loading and training loop omitted for brevity ...
    for epoch in range(config["max_epochs"]):
        # train one epoch ...
        val_acc = 0.0  # placeholder: compute on validation set
        # Send the *intermediate* score to Ray Tune every epoch.
        # The ASHA scheduler uses these to decide whether to kill this trial.
        tune.report(mean_accuracy=val_acc)

# ─── 2. Configure the ASHA scheduler ──────────────────────────────────────────
asha = ASHAScheduler(
    time_attr="training_iteration",   # one unit = one epoch
    max_t=50,                         # max epochs (R)
    grace_period=2,                   # don't kill before epoch 2
    reduction_factor=3,               # eta = 3
    brackets=3,                       # number of Hyperband brackets (s_max + 1)
)

# ─── 3. Use HyperOpt (TPE) as the search algorithm → that's BOHB-style ────────
search = HyperOptSearch(metric="mean_accuracy", mode="max", n_initial_points=10)

# ─── 4. Run the search ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
analysis = tune.run(
    train_fn,
    config={
        "lr":              tune.loguniform(1e-4, 1e-1),
        "batch_size":      tune.choice([32, 64, 128, 256]),
        "conv1_channels":  tune.choice([16, 32, 64]),
        "conv2_channels":  tune.choice([32, 64, 128]),
        "dropout":         tune.uniform(0.0, 0.5),
        "max_epochs":      50,
    },
    num_samples=64,            # how many configs to sample overall
    scheduler=asha,
    search_alg=search,
    metric="mean_accuracy",
    mode="max",
    resources_per_trial={"cpu": 4, "gpu": 0.5},
    name="asha_cnn_fashionmnist",
)

print("Best trial config:", analysis.best_config)
print("Best trial final accuracy:", analysis.best_result["mean_accuracy"])

The two things to notice in this snippet: first, tune.report(...) is called every epoch, which is what the scheduler uses to make its kill decisions — without intermediate reports, ASHA cannot prune and degenerates to plain random search. Second, grace_period controls how many epochs you train before any pruning is allowed — setting this too low is dangerous because early epochs are noisy and you can kill configs that would have caught up. grace_period=2 or 5 is typical for image classification; for NLP fine-tuning with very long schedules, grace_period of 10+ is common.

8. When to Use Hyperband (and When Not To)

Hyperband shines when three conditions hold simultaneously. First, you have a meaningful budget axis — a hyperparameter (or a training duration) that you can vary to trade compute for evaluation quality. n_estimators for tree ensembles, epochs for neural networks, n_iter for SGD-based linear models, even subsample for stochastic gradient boosting all qualify. Second, the budget-quality relationship is monotone-or-near-monotone: more budget never makes a configuration dramatically worse, so partial-budget scores are a useful proxy for full-budget scores. Third, the candidates you are searching over have substantial variance in quality — if all candidates are nearly equally good, the elimination step provides no signal and you have paid Hyperband's overhead for nothing.

When these conditions hold, Hyperband typically delivers 3-10x speedup over single-fidelity search at the same final-model quality. When they do not — for example, when your "budget" axis is a discrete switch with no natural ordering, or when all candidates are within 1% of each other on the metric — Hyperband adds complexity without benefit, and plain RandomizedSearchCV or GridSearchCV is simpler and equally fast.

There is one important caveat about the monotonicity assumption. Hyperband assumes that early-budget scores are a noisy but unbiased estimator of late-budget scores. If your model has a long warmup phase (learning rate warmup, transfer-learning feature extraction, etc.), early scores may be systematically misleading — a configuration that looks bad at epoch 2 may be the best at epoch 50. The fix is the grace_period parameter (in Ray Tune) or min_resources (in sklearn): make the smallest budget large enough that the warmup is over before any pruning happens.

🧠 Quick Check

You are tuning an XGBoost model on a 5M-row tabular dataset. n_estimators=2000 takes about 4 minutes per fit. Your search space has 6 hyperparameters and you want to try roughly 500 candidates. Estimate the wall-clock time of (a) RandomizedSearchCV with 3-fold CV, and (b) HalvingRandomSearchCV with the same total compute budget and η=3.

Show Answer

(a) RandomizedSearchCV: 500 candidates × 3 folds × 4 min = 6000 min = 100 hours. (b) HalvingRandomSearchCV spends the same total budget B = 100 hours, but distributes it across brackets: most candidates are evaluated at small n_estimators (e.g. 50-100 trees, ~15-30 seconds per fit), and only the survivors make it to 2000 trees. In practice Halving finishes in 10-25 hours — a 4-10x speedup. The exact factor depends on how many bad candidates exist; the more variance in candidate quality, the bigger the Hyperband win.

🎯 Key Takeaways

  • Multi-fidelity means treating each candidate's score as a curve against training budget, not a single number — most bad configs reveal themselves early.
  • Successive Halving keeps top 1/η of candidates each round and gives each survivor η× more budget; total compute scales as R · log_η(n) instead of n · R.
  • Hyperband runs s_max + 1 SHA brackets with different n values, sidestepping the exploration-exploitation trade-off that plain SHA forces you to commit to.
  • BOHB replaces Hyperband's random sampling with TPE/BO proposals, combining multi-fidelity speed with Bayesian sample efficiency.
  • scikit-learn: HalvingRandomSearchCV and HalvingGridSearchCV (since 1.0, experimental) for tree ensembles where n_estimators is the budget.
  • Ray Tune: ASHAScheduler is the production-grade distributed variant for deep learning; pair with HyperOptSearch for BOHB-style behavior.
  • Use Hyperband when you have a monotone budget axis, candidates vary substantially in quality, and per-candidate full training is expensive; otherwise plain random search is simpler.
